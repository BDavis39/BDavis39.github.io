The Sensitivity of Boundary Layer Characteristics to Entrainment-Enhanced Buoynant Mixing and Surface Evaporative Fraction

In the absence of background circulations, the land surface can influence atmospheric characteristics that affect cloud formation, precipitation, and weather extremes. The portion of the atmosphere influenced, in one hour or less, by the land surface is defined as the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL). The ABL is variable in height and invisible. However, in instances where clouds form, the ABL height can be inferred as its growth provides the conditions for cloud formation. When the land surface is the primary driver of ABL growth, and thus cloud formation, it is said that the land and atmosphere (L-A) are coupled. Therefore, evaluating the growth of the ABL can give insight into the land surface conditions that produced the growth when L-A coupling is happening. Previously, lack of observations meant that numerical models were used to simulate ABL growth. The region a model can simulate is limited by computing resources. So, this research seeks to extend a method that can determine cloud base height and link it to the surface state. The extension adds in an aspect of ABL growth that would make the estimation of the cloud base height more accurate. To ensure the method is a viable substitution, it is compared to output from a numerical model. The analysis is conducted in a region known for having strong L-A coupling over a wide range of surface and atmospheric conditions to establish the utility of the method for estimated ABL growth and thus surface conditions.
